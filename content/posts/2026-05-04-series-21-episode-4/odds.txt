## The Strategy
If I was Ed Gamble are looking to place bets on outcomes

- Placing a bet on Amy being crowned series champion has the `r 70.65/(100-70.65)`
 
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{P}(\text{Amy winning the series}) &= 70.65\% \\
\mathbb{P}(\text{Amy NOT winning the series}) &= 1 - \mathbb{P}(\text{Amy winning the series}) \\
&= 100\% - 70.65\% = 29.35\% \\
\text{Odds of Amy Winning the Series} &= \frac{\mathbb{P}(\text{Amy winning the series})}{\mathbb{P}(\text{Amy NOT winning the series})} = 2.41
\end{aligned}
$$

In a naive betting scenario with British fractional odds in which the return from a bet is exactly the gambled amount multiplied by the odds with no additional fees, if I was to place a bet of £100 on Amy winning:

- **If Amy does win**, I will receive £`r 100*2.41` as a return for my bet, and the profit would be £`r (100*2.41)-100`. 
    - This is expected to occur with 70.65% probability.
- **If Amy doesn't win**, I will receive £0, and the profit would be £`r 0-100`, that is, I would have incurred a loss of £100. 
    - This is expected to occur with 29.35% chance.

The expected profit from this bet would be:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}(\text{Profit on Betting that Amy Will Win the Series}) \\ &= \text{Profit from Amy winning the Series} \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{Amy winning the series}) \\  &+\text{Profit from Amy NOT winning the Series} \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{Amy NOT winning the series}) \\
&= £141 \times 70.65\% + (-£100) \times 29.35\% \\
&= £70.27
\end{aligned}
$$
Betting on Amy would lead to a positive profit in the long run.

### Odds, Returns and Profits
Let $A$ be the event that we are are interested in betting on; for example, Amy being crowned champion for Series 21, or Armando coming in 5th place for the series.

Then, we define $\mathbb{P}(A)$ to be the probability associated with event $A$ occurring. 

We use $A^c$ and $\mathbb{P}(A^c)$ to denote the complement of event $A$ and the probability of this complement event. The complement, $^c$, represents event $A$ **not** occurring, and consequently, $\mathbb{P}(A^c) = 1 - \mathbb{P}(A)$.

The [odds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odds) [^odds] associated with event $A$, $\mathbb{O}(A)$ is defined as:



[^odds]: Fractional odds are the common way to represent bets in the UK, and as Taskmaster UK is a British show, let's 
$$
\begin{aligned}

\mathbb{O}(A) &= \text{Odds of Event } A \text{ occuring} \\ 
&= \frac{\mathbb{P}(A)}{\mathbb{P}(A^c)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(A)}{1- \mathbb{P}(A)} 
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Let }&M \text{ be the monetary amount placed on betting on Event } A \\


\text{The return on the bet if Event } A \text{ does occurr is} \\
R_A = M \cdot \mathbb{O}(A) \\

\text{If Event } A \text{ does not not occur, then we receive 0.} \\

\end{aligned}

$$
The return on making a bet associated with Event A 
$$
Q_A = P_A - M = 
\begin{cases} 
      M \cdot \mathbb{O}(A) - M  = M \cdot [ \mathbb{O}(A) -M] &\quad \text{if } A \text{ does occur} \\
      0 - M = -M&\quad \text{if } A \text{ does NOT occur}
\end{cases}

$$

The associated profits, $Q_A$ 
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{The associated profits are } \\

Q_A = \text{Profit if Event A occurs} &= R_A - M \\
&= M \cdot \mathbb{O}(A) - M \\
&=   M \cdot [\mathbb{O}(A) - 1] \\

\end{aligned}
$$
